# Overview

Traditional economics treats intelligence as an implicit component of labor.

The emergence of AI systems introduces intelligence as an independently deployable, scalable, composable, and potentially measurable production factor.

This RFC explores whether future execution systems may require mechanisms to estimate:

- Intelligence contribution
- Intelligence efficiency
- Cost per intelligence unit
- Effective intelligence utilization
- Governed intelligence contribution

---

# Core Observation

Historically:

    Labor
      ↓
    Intelligence
      ↓
    Execution
      ↓
    Outcome

Intelligence was inseparable from labor.

Today:

    Human Intelligence
    AI Intelligence
    Hybrid Intelligence
          ↓
      Execution
          ↓
       Outcome

Intelligence can increasingly be acquired, deployed, scaled, and consumed independently.

This may require new economic models.

---

# Key Insight

The Information Economy focused on information.

The AI Economy focuses on intelligence generation.

The Execution Economy may ultimately focus on the transformation of intelligence into outcomes.

Intelligence alone does not create value.

Value emerges through execution.

---

# Proposed Concepts

## Raw Intelligence

Potential decision-making capability.

## Effective Intelligence

Intelligence that contributes to successful execution outcomes.

## Governed Intelligence

Intelligence operating within:

- Policies
- Permissions
- Governance controls
- Trust boundaries
- Execution constraints

Governed intelligence may be economically more valuable than unrestricted intelligence.

## Intelligence Unit (IU)

A future abstract measurement unit representing normalized intelligence contribution.

## Effective Intelligence Unit (EIU)

An estimated intelligence contribution adjusted for:

- Outcome quality
- Execution success
- Governance compliance
- Beneficiary value

---

# Intelligence as a Latent Variable

Intelligence is not directly observable.

Instead, systems observe:

- Decisions
- Executions
- Outcomes
- Corrections
- Escalations
- Retries
- Approvals
- Artifacts

Similar to latent state estimation in probabilistic systems, intelligence contribution may need to be inferred from observable behavior.

Potential influences:

- Hidden Markov Models
- Latent Variable Models
- Bayesian Estimation
- Econometric Models

# Summary

The emergence of independently deployable intelligence may require new economic models.

Traditional economics measures:

- Labor
- Capital
- Energy
- Time

Future execution economies may additionally estimate:

- Intelligence
- Effective Intelligence
- Intelligence Efficiency
- Cost per Intelligence Unit

The central question becomes:

    How much intelligence is consumed
    to produce a unit of value?
